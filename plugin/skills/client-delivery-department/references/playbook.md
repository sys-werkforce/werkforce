1. New-payer sweep. At every session open, read `company/customers/` for names at `paid` with no onboarding row on the board. Each new name gets a brief and a Filed row the same day - the most expensive silence in any company is the one right after money changes hands. (candidate)

2. Activation before onboarding. Before writing a single onboarding step, name the observable customer behavior that means they got first real value - a file opened, a tool used, a result seen. If activation cannot be observed, redefine it until it can; the steps come after. (candidate)

3. Checkpoint-shaped steps. Every onboarding step ends in a checkpoint someone can confirm happened - a reply received, a call held, a login seen - with an expected date. A step that ends in "they should understand it" is not a step; rewrite it until it is confirmable. (candidate)

4. The honesty read. Each session, compare every open `Promised:` line in the customer book against real evidence - a worklog receipt, an outbox file, a dated History line. Report each promise on track, behind, or done, with a claim label. Behind gets said early, in a draft the founder can send. (candidate)

5. Handover as a file. Every finished deliverable becomes one outbox file naming what was produced, where it lives, and what the customer does next. The customer-facing note travels with it as a draft - the founder sends, always. (candidate)

6. Signals, not vibes. Watch for churn as observable behavior - a checkpoint missed, a check-in unanswered, something they paid for unused for a stated window - and pair every signal with a recommended response. The watchlist itself is crafted with customer-care; this department runs it. (candidate)

7. Slip protocol. The moment a due date will be missed, the customer hears it from us before they have to ask: draft the update with the new date, what changed, and what we are doing about it, then queue it for the founder to send. Bad news early is a service; bad news late is churn. (candidate)
